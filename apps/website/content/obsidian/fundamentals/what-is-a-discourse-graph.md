## What is a discourse graph?

**Discourse Graphs** are an information model that emphasizes discourse moves (such as **questions**, **claims**, and **evidence**), and relations (such as **support** or **opposition**) as the main units of a body of knowledge.

![](/docs/obsidian/dg-schema02.png)
_The discourse graph conceptual schema_

Below is an image of discourse graph investigating a question from the metascience literature: _"Is citation count a hopelessly confounded metric?"_

The **question** is attributed to its ultimate source, a 1968 paper by Robert K. Merton that christened the phenomenon of cumulative advantage as the "Matthew Effect." As you can see it also references **claims** and **evidence** from more recent work produced in conversation with Merton's seminal paper. The author of this graph is curating the most interesting claims and strongest evidence from each of these sources as a predicate for their own original research contributions on the topic.

![alt text](/docs/obsidian/metascience-dg-example.png)
_A discourse graph from a literature synthesis. Note how each evidence node is tightly integrated with its source for ease of provenancing_

## Why use a discourse graph?

The discourse graph supports reasoning and retrieval based on the discourse units that are central to scientific conversation and discovery

### Understanding a problem

The researcher assembling the discourse graph above is mapping out a problem space in preparation for making an original research contribution, curating the most interesting claims and strongest evidence related to his or her original research question, rather than remaining constrained by the research questions motivating the papers from which the claims and evidence derive.

### Formulating a research contribution

The form of the discourse graph makes it easier to spot weak links and gaps in the evidentiary support network undergirding a claim. This analysis can be applied to existing bodies of literature as well as to assessing whether a current research project has made a sufficient contribution.

![alt text](/docs/obsidian/metascience-dg-example02.png)
_Formulating experiments to test a claim from the literature_

### Constructing a theory

Discourse graphs can be used to visually assess the structure of a theory or model: are the edges well-positioned? Sufficiently evidenced? Necessary?

Consider how that information model foregrounds the conceptual building blocks and relationships that are important for synthesis, compared to a typical "[iTunes for papers](http://joelchan.me/assets/pdf/2019-cscw-beyond-itunes-for-papers.pdf)" model that foregrounds documents in a way that forces us to drudge through [tedious extraction work](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3295750.3298937) before we can do the thinking we want to do!

![](/docs/obsidian/bans-hate-speech.png)

This information model (theoretically!) has high potential for augmenting individual and collective "research-grade" synthesis (e.g. lit reviews for a dissertation or grant proposal).

Discourse Graphs are not a new idea (you can read more about it in [this short (academic-focused) write-up](http://joelchan.me/assets/pdf/Discourse_Graphs_for_Augmented_Knowledge_Synthesis_What_and_Why.pdf) or this more [practically-oriented article](https://oasislab.pubpub.org/pub/54t0y9mk/release/3)), but the potential to use it for everyday research work is!
